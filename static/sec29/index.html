Definition: Inverse functions

Two functions \(f\) and \(g\) are inverse functions of each other if both statement \[(f \circ g)(x) = x\] and \[(g \circ f)(x) = x\] are true.

If one of the statements is true but the other fails, then \(f\) and \(g\) are called partial inverses of each other.

Example

\(f(x) = \frac{x-4}{3} + 1\). Find \(f^{-1}(x)\).

Computational Approach

We begin by writing the computational steps taken to evaluate \(f\), and then write down the opposite operations in the reverse order for \(f^{-1}\).

\(f:\)

  1. subtract 4
  2. divide by 3
  3. add 1

\(f^{-1}:\)

  1. subtract 1
  2. multiply by 3
  3. add 4

\[f^{-1}(x) = 3(x-1) + 4 = 3x + 1\]

Algebraic Approach

We write out \(f\) as an equation \(y = f(x)\), then we reverse the roles of the \(x\) and \(y\) variables to get an equation for \(f^{-1}\) and solve that equation for \(y\) to get the formula for \(f^{-1}(x)\).

\[\begin{align*} f &: y = \frac{x-4}{3} + 1 \\ f^{-1} &: x = \frac{y-4}{3} + 1 \\ & x - 1 = \frac{y-4}{3} \\ & 3(x-1) = y - 4 \\ & 3(x-1) + 4 = y \end{align*}\] \[f^{-1}(x) = 3(x-1) + 4 = 3x + 1\]

Graphs of Inverse Functions

Notice how in the algebraic approach to the above example we swap the role of the \(x\) and \(y\) variables in order to find the inverse function. This has a graphical interpretation: swap the \(x\) and \(y\) coordinates of points in order to graph the inverse function.

In other words, if, say \((2,7)\) and \((5,-3)\) are points on the graph of \(y = f(x)\), then \((7,2)\) and \((-3,5)\) will be points on the graph of \(y = f^{-1}(x).\)

Example

\(f(x) = \frac{x^3}{10} + x\). Graph \(y = f(x)\) and \(y = f^{-1}(x)\) on the same coordinate plane.

We make a table of points in order to graph \(f(x)\) \[\begin{array}{c | c} x & f(x) \\ \hline -3 & -5.7 \\ -2 & -2.8 \\ -1 & -1.1 \\ 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 1.1 \\ 2 & 2.8 \\ 3 & 5.7 \end{array}\] Now draw the graph.

In order to graph \(f^{-1}(x)\), we swap the \(x\) and \(y\) coordinates on our table. A table for \(f^{-1}(x)\) then looks like: \[\begin{array}{c | c} x & f^{-1}(x) \\ \hline -5.7 & -3 \\ -2.8 & -2 \\ -1.1 & -1 \\ 0 & 0 \\ 1.1 & 1 \\ 2.8 & 2 \\ 5.7 & 3 \end{array}\] Now draw the graph of \(f^{-1}(x)\) on the same coordinate plane.

The Graph of \(f^{-1}(x)\) is a reflection of the graph of \(f(x)\)

Do the example above: you will see an interesting pattern.

You should have seen that the graph of \(f^{1}(x)\) is the reflection of the graph of \(f(x)\) across the line \(y = x\) (line with slope \(1\) passing through \((0,0)\), draw it).

This is always true: to find the graph of \(f^{-1}(x)\), take the graph of \(f(x)\) and reflect it across the line \(y = x\).

Example

Say \(f(x) = x^2\) and \(g(x) = \sqrt{x}\). Show why \(f\) and \(g\) are partial inverses, but they are not full inverses.

To check to see if two functions are inverses, compose them.

\[(f \circ g)(x) = (\sqrt{x})^2 = x.\] Composition in this directions gives \(x\), as desired.

\[(g \circ f)(x) = \sqrt{x^2} = |x|.\] Composition in this direction doesn't give \(x\), it gives \(|x|\).

Because one direction fails to give \(x\), the functions are not inverses, they are only partial inverses.

Graphical Explanation of Partial Inverses

We can see why \(f(x) = x^2\) fails to have a full inverse by examining the graph.

We graph \(f(x) = x^2\). Here's a table to get you started. \[\begin{array}{c | c} x & y \\ \hline -2 & 4 \\ -1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 4 \end{array}\] Now go ahead and graph that.

What happens if we swap all the \(x\) and \(y\) coordinates in order to draw the graph of the inverse? We get \[\begin{array}{c | c} x & y \\ \hline 4 & -2 \\ 1 & -1 \\ 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \\ 4 & 2 \end{array}\] Now graph that on a separate coordinate plane.

Is that graph even a function? Does it pass the vertical line test?

Since the resulting graph is not a function, we can't successfully create a full inverse for \(f(x) = x^2\). The best we can do is create a partial inverse, which is what the graph of \(\sqrt{x}\) is.

Here's a table for \(y = \sqrt{x}\). \[\begin{array}{c | c} x & \sqrt{x} \\ \hline 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \\ 4 & 2 \end{array}\] Graph that sucker. You'll see that it's basically half of the graph of what we got when we flipped \(y = x^2\), in a way that makes the result a function.

So you see, \(\sqrt{x}\) is an inverse for about half of \(x^2\), so that's why we call it a partial inverse.

Horizontal Line Test

The above discussion suggests to us a way where we can determine whether a function has a full inverse or a partial inverse just by looking at the graph: we call this method the horizontal line test

Take a function \(f(x)\) and look at its graph. If you can draw a horizontal line (remember, "horizontal" as in "the horizon") that hits your graph in more than one place, then \(f(x)\) will fail to have a full inverse.

For example, the graph of \(f(x) = x^2\) does not pass the horizontal line test, so we can tell that \(x^2\) won't have a full inverse.

Any graph that does pass the horizontal line test will have a full inverse. We call graphs that pass the horizontal line test one-to-one functions.
